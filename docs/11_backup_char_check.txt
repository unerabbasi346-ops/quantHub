All future documents shall reference this architecture but shall not redefine, override, or duplicate it. Amendments require explicit governance approval and a new document version.

Date of freeze: 2026-06-30

---

## Foundational Architecture (Parts 1–6)

### F-1: Bronze-Silver-Gold Medallion Architecture
The data platform uses a three-layer storage model: Bronze (raw source data, immutable after receipt), Silver (validated, normalized, schema-compliant), Gold (business-ready, optimized for analytics and model consumption). Downstream layers shall never modify upstream data. Every dataset possesses a globally unique identifier and version.

### F-2: Strategy Independence
The data platform is entirely strategy-agnostic. No ingestion pipeline, transformation, storage schema, validation rule, metadata model, or governance mechanism shall assume the existence of any specific trading strategy. Lancaster participates as one governed strategy implementation without introducing architecture-specific behavior.

### F-3: Immutable Dataset Publication
Published datasets are immutable after publication. Corrections create new governed dataset versions rather than modifying published records. Dataset versioning is mandatory for all governed assets.

### F-4: Event-Driven Pipeline Orchestration
Every pipeline transition, validation result, lifecycle state change, publication event, and operational event is communicated through standardized event contracts. The Event Platform is the primary integration mechanism across Quant Hub.

### F-5: Cloud-Neutral Architecture
The architecture is vendor-independent and cloud-neutral. Storage architecture remains independent of cloud providers, object storage vendors, filesystem implementations, database vendors, and distributed storage frameworks. Migration between technologies shall not require redesign of higher architectural layers.

### F-6: Full Auditability
Every data operation generates immutable audit records. Complete audit trails support reconstruction of any published dataset. Audit records are retained according to enterprise retention policies.

## Part 7 — Enterprise Data Storage Architecture

### 7.1: Enterprise Data Lake Architecture

#### D-7.1.1: Authoritative Repository
The Enterprise Data Lake is the single authoritative repository for all persistent enterprise data assets within Quant Hub. No production dataset shall exist outside the scope of the data lake.

#### D-7.1.2: Storage Zone Model
Storage is organized into governed zones with explicit data classification, access controls, and lifecycle policies per zone.

#### D-7.1.3: Storage Independence
Storage interfaces remain independent of physical storage implementations. Computational services interact through standardized storage interfaces. Physical storage details are abstracted from consuming applications.

#### D-7.1.4: Dataset Registration Mandate
Every dataset shall be registered in the enterprise catalog before entering any governed storage zone. No unregistered dataset shall persist within governed storage.

---

### 7.2: Enterprise Lakehouse Architecture

#### D-7.2.1: Compute-Storage Separation
Computation and storage are architecturally separated. Compute engines interact with storage through standardized interfaces. Storage is never embedded within computational services.

#### D-7.2.2: ACID Compliance
The Lakehouse provides ACID-compliant transaction semantics across analytical workloads. Multi-Version Concurrency Control (MVCC) enables concurrent reads and writes without blocking.

#### D-7.2.3: Snapshot Management and Time Travel
The Lakehouse supports governed snapshot management enabling deterministic time-travel queries. Every dataset mutation produces a new snapshot with explicit version identifiers. Historical snapshots are preserved according to enterprise retention policies.

#### D-7.2.4: Processing Isolation
Batch, streaming, and incremental processing workloads execute in isolated compute environments while sharing governed storage. No workload type shall interfere with the operational characteristics of another.

---

### 7.3: Storage Engines & File Formats

#### D-7.3.1: Vendor Independence
Storage architecture remains independent of cloud providers, object storage vendors, filesystem implementations, database vendors, and distributed storage frameworks.

#### D-7.3.2: Computational Independence
Persistent storage shall never embed assumptions regarding query engines, machine learning frameworks, research platforms, feature engineering pipelines, or strategy engines. All computational services interact through standardized storage interfaces.

#### D-7.3.3: Format Selection Governance
File format selection is governed through enterprise standards. Format decisions consider analytical performance, compression efficiency, schema evolution support, and ecosystem compatibility.

---

### 7.4: Data Lifecycle & Retention Architecture

#### D-7.4.1: Lifecycle State Model
Every governed dataset progresses through defined lifecycle states: Draft, Validating, Published, Active, Archived, Legal Hold, Retired, Destroyed. State transitions are deterministic, governed, and fully auditable.

#### D-7.4.2: Immutable Published Assets
Published datasets remain immutable. Corrections create new versions. This principle applies to data content and metadata alike.

#### D-7.4.3: Legal Hold Architecture
Legal Hold suspends standard lifecycle progression including archival, retirement, and destruction. Hold activation, duration, and release are governed through formal approval workflows.

#### D-7.4.4: Secure Destruction
Dataset destruction requires formal authorization. Destruction is irreversible and fully auditable. Destruction scope includes all copies, backups, and derived assets where legally permissible.

#### D-7.4.5: Retention Policy Hierarchy
Retention policies follow a hierarchical model: regulatory requirements take precedence over business policies, which take precedence over operational defaults. No asset may be destroyed while any retention obligation remains unsatisfied.

---

### 7.5: Backup & Disaster Recovery Architecture

#### D-7.5.1: Backup is a Governance Process
Backup is not an operational afterthought — it is a governed process with defined policies, verification procedures, and audit trails.

#### D-7.5.2: Complete Platform Preservation
Backup includes all information required to reconstruct enterprise analytical operations: raw/curated/published datasets, feature repositories, metadata services, catalogs, lineage graphs, configuration, workflow definitions, security metadata, and audit repositories.

#### D-7.5.3: Immutable Backup Copies
Approved backup sets become immutable after successful creation. Immutability protects against accidental modification, operational errors, insider threats, malware, ransomware, and unauthorized administrative activity.

#### D-7.5.4: Independent Recovery
Recovery procedures operate independently from production infrastructure. Recovery remains possible following complete infrastructure replacement.

#### D-7.5.5: Continuous Verification
Every backup undergoes automated integrity verification. Verification confirms completeness, correctness, recoverability, and immutability.

---

### 7.6: Data Archiving & Cold Storage Architecture

#### D-7.6.1: Archive Does Not Mean Delete
Archival is distinct from deletion. Archived datasets remain discoverable, governed, and recoverable. Storage optimization never becomes the primary objective. Preservation of enterprise information takes precedence over storage cost reduction.

#### D-7.6.2: Metadata First
Metadata remains immediately accessible even when archived datasets reside in deep storage. Enterprise discovery services never require physical restoration simply to identify archived assets.

#### D-7.6.3: Immutable Historical Preservation
Archived datasets remain immutable. Historical information is never modified after archival. Corrections create new governed dataset versions rather than modifying archived records.

#### D-7.6.4: Four-Tier Storage Model
Storage is organized into four tiers: Tier 0 (Active Operational), Tier 1 (Warm), Tier 2 (Cold), Tier 3 (Deep Archive). Tier transitions are policy-driven and governed.

#### D-7.6.5: Deterministic Retrieval
Every archived dataset possesses a deterministic retrieval path. Recovery preserves dataset identity, version identifiers, schema, metadata, lineage, and integrity verification evidence.

---

### 7.7: Metadata & Catalog Services Architecture

#### D-7.7.1: Metadata is an Enterprise Asset
Metadata is governed with the same rigor as the data it describes. Metadata governance is not optional.

#### D-7.7.2: Single Source of Truth
The Enterprise Metadata Registry is the single authoritative source of platform metadata. No subsystem shall maintain independent authoritative metadata outside the Registry.

#### D-7.7.3: Metadata Before Storage
No dataset enters governed storage without first being registered in the Enterprise Catalog. Metadata registration precedes physical storage operations.

#### D-7.7.4: Immutable Historical Metadata
Historical metadata remains immutable after publication. Changes affecting governed assets generate new metadata versions rather than modifying historical records.

#### D-7.7.5: Continuous Synchronization
Metadata remains synchronized with enterprise assets throughout their lifecycle. Synchronization failures generate operational alerts and governance review.

#### D-7.7.6: Metadata Domain Model
Metadata is organized into domains: Identity, Ownership, Lifecycle, Governance, Operational, and Relationship information. Every governed dataset possesses complete metadata across all applicable domains.

---

### 7.8: Data Lineage Architecture

#### D-7.8.1: Complete Traceability
Every governed data asset possesses complete end-to-end lineage. Lineage captures data origin, transformation, movement, validation, storage, and consumption.

#### D-7.8.2: Immutable History
Historical lineage remains immutable after publication. Lineage events are never retroactively modified.

#### D-7.8.3: Deterministic Relationships
Lineage relationships are deterministic. The lineage graph accurately represents actual data flows without approximation.

#### D-7.8.4: Lineage Granularity
Lineage is captured at multiple granularities: Dataset, Table, File, Partition, Column, Feature, Model, Pipeline, and Strategy levels.

#### D-7.8.5: Technology Independence
Lineage architecture remains independent from storage engines, processing frameworks, and infrastructure technologies.

---

### 7.9: Data Quality Architecture

#### D-7.9.1: Quality by Design
Quality validation occurs throughout the data lifecycle rather than only after ingestion. Quality is a first-class platform capability, not an optional validation process.

#### D-7.9.2: Continuous Validation
Quality is continuously monitored rather than periodically inspected. Validation executes during ingestion, pipeline processing, pre-publication, on schedule, on events, and on demand.

#### D-7.9.3: Automated Enforcement
Quality policies are enforced automatically. Manual quality enforcement is minimized.

#### D-7.9.4: Immutable Quality Evidence
Quality reports are immutable following publication. Historical validation evidence remains permanently available for governance purposes.

#### D-7.9.5: Standardized Quality Dimensions
Every governed dataset is evaluated across standardized dimensions: Accuracy, Completeness, Consistency, Timeliness, Validity, Integrity, Uniqueness, Availability, Traceability, and Compliance.

#### D-7.9.6: Deterministic Quality Scoring
Quality scores provide consistent, reproducible assessments derived from complete validation evidence. Every quality score is reproducible using identical datasets, validation rules, and quality policies. Scores never replace detailed validation reports.

#### D-7.9.7: Validation Severity Classification
Validation findings are classified by severity: Informational, Advisory, Warning, Error, Critical. Severity determines escalation paths and certification consequences.

#### D-7.9.8: Quality Certification Framework
Every production dataset requires formal quality certification. Certification gates prevent promotion of non-compliant datasets. Certification records are immutable.

---

### 7.10: Data Contracts Architecture

#### D-7.10.1: Contract-First Design
Every data interface is defined through a formal contract before implementation begins. Contracts serve as the source of truth for interface expectations.

#### D-7.10.2: Independent Producer and Consumer Evolution
Producers and consumers evolve independently. Contracts define the interface boundary. Contract compatibility is explicitly declared and governed.

#### D-7.10.3: Immutable Contract Versions
Published contract versions remain immutable. Modifications create new contract versions with explicit version identifiers. Version compatibility follows semantic versioning: Major (breaking), Minor (backward-compatible additions), Patch (backward-compatible corrections).

#### D-7.10.4: Automated Enforcement
Contract compliance is verified automatically. Enforcement mechanisms include Publication Gates, Consumption Gates, Quality Gates, Lifecycle Gates, and Integration Gates. Enforcement never silently degrades.

#### D-7.10.5: Contract Components
Every contract includes: Schema Definition, Semantic Definition, Quality Requirements, Availability Requirements, Performance Requirements, Security Requirements, Lifecycle Requirements, Governance Requirements, Ownership Information, and Version Information.

#### D-7.10.6: Technology Independence
Contracts remain independent of storage engines, serialization formats, processing frameworks, and infrastructure technologies.

---

### 7.11: Data Governance Architecture

#### D-7.11.1: Governance by Default
Every data asset is governed. Assets are explicitly exempted from governance rather than requiring explicit inclusion. No data asset, policy, or process operates outside enterprise governance.

#### D-7.11.2: Policy as Code
Governance policies are defined, versioned, and tested as code. Policy definitions are machine-readable and automatically enforceable.

#### D-7.11.3: Separation of Duties
Policy definition, policy enforcement, and policy audit are separated. No individual role possesses unrestricted governance authority.

#### D-7.11.4: Continuous Compliance
Compliance is continuously verified rather than periodically audited. Compliance violations trigger automated remediation workflows.

#### D-7.11.5: Immutable Governance Evidence
Governance decisions produce immutable audit records. Historical governance evidence remains permanently available.

#### D-7.11.6: Formal Stewardship
Every governed dataset has a formally designated Data Steward. Stewardship assignments are recorded in the enterprise metadata registry. Stewardship responsibilities include ownership, metadata management, quality oversight, and access approval.

#### D-7.11.7: Exception Management
Governance exceptions are time-bound and require formal justification, risk assessment, and periodic review. Exceptions never persist indefinitely.

---

### 7.12: Data Security Architecture

#### D-7.12.1: Security by Design
Security is incorporated during architecture design rather than added after implementation. Every component is designed with security controls from inception.

#### D-7.12.2: Defense in Depth
Multiple independent security controls protect every data asset. No single control failure shall compromise security.

#### D-7.12.3: Zero Trust Architecture
No component, service, or identity is inherently trusted. Every access request is explicitly authenticated and authorized.

#### D-7.12.4: Least Privilege
Every identity possesses only the permissions necessary for its authorized functions. Privileges are periodically reviewed and reduced where possible.

#### D-7.12.5: Encryption Mandate
All governed data is encrypted at rest. All data transmission is encrypted. Enterprise key management governs key generation, storage, rotation, revocation, and access.

#### D-7.12.6: Data Classification
Every data asset is classified according to enterprise security classification standards: Public, Internal, Confidential, Restricted, Regulated. Classification determines applicable security controls and is recorded in the metadata registry.

#### D-7.12.7: Default-Deny Failure Mode
Security service failure defaults to deny rather than allow. Availability architecture ensures no individual security component constitutes a single point of failure.

---

### 7.13: Data Observability Architecture

#### D-7.13.1: Observability by Design
Every platform component emits observability data from inception. Observability is not retrofitted after implementation.

#### D-7.13.2: Unified Observability Model
Metrics, logs, and traces follow standardized models across all components and are correlated through common identifiers.

#### D-7.13.3: Proactive Detection
Anomalies are detected before they affect downstream consumers. Detection is automated rather than dependent on manual inspection.

#### D-7.13.4: Immutable Observability Evidence
Operational data is immutable after recording. Historical observability evidence supports audit and investigation.

#### D-7.13.5: Service Level Objectives
Every critical service has defined SLOs covering availability, latency, throughput, error rate, durability, freshness, and recovery. SLOs are continuously measured. SLO violations trigger operational alerts and incident management.

#### D-7.13.6: Intelligent Alerting
Alerting includes threshold-based, anomaly-based, trend-based, and composite alerts with severity classification, routing, escalation, suppression, and resolution. Alert fatigue is actively managed.

#### D-7.13.7: Technology Independence
Observability architecture remains independent of monitored technologies, storage engines, and infrastructure platforms. Observability service failure shall not affect monitored platform services.

## Architectural Invariants (Cross-Cutting)

These principles appear across multiple sections and are considered architectural invariants. No future document may redefine or contradict them.

### I-1: Immutability After Publication
Data, metadata, lineage, quality reports, governance decisions, audit records, backup copies, archived datasets, contracts, and observability evidence are all immutable after publication. Corrections create new versions. This invariant spans sections 7.1 through 7.13.

### I-2: Technology Independence
Storage, processing, governance, quality, security, contracts, lineage, metadata, and observability architectures all remain independent of specific technology implementations. This enables vendor migration without architectural redesign.

### I-3: Strategy Independence
No data platform component shall assume the existence of any specific trading strategy. The platform serves all strategies equally. Strategy-specific configurations are external to the platform architecture.

### I-4: Automated Enforcement
Manual enforcement of policies, quality rules, contracts, governance, and security is minimized. Automation is the default enforcement mechanism across all architectural domains.

### I-5: Continuous Verification
Validation, compliance, monitoring, quality assessment, backup integrity, and security posture are verified continuously rather than periodically. Proactive detection takes precedence over reactive response.

### I-6: Complete Auditability
Every governed action produces immutable audit evidence. Audit trails support complete reconstruction of platform history. Audit records are retained according to enterprise retention policies.

### I-7: No Bypass Architecture
No governed component, service, or path operates outside its designated governance, security, quality, or operational framework. Silently degraded enforcement is prohibited. Violations produce explicit, auditable evidence.
